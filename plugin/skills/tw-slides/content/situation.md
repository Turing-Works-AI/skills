# Content Guide: Situation Slide

These guidelines tier down from the slide craft rules in SKILL.md. They govern what goes on this slide, not how it looks. For visual spec, see `components/situation/spec.md`.

---

## Purpose

The situation slide establishes where the client is today. It answers: "What is the current state, and why does it matter?" This slide builds the case for action — everything that follows (objectives, workplan, proposal) rests on this foundation.

---

## Section subtitle (left panel)

Format: `"Understanding [Client]'s [domain] context and [aspiration]"`

Examples from actual decks:
- "Understanding Lyka's context and AI ambitions"
- Should name the client and signal the domain being addressed

---

## Action title (bold teal headline)

The action title is the single most important sentence on the slide. It states the client's current position and what they are seeking — written as a conclusion, not a topic label.

**Pattern:** `"[Client] is [doing/seeking/facing] [specific thing] across [scope]"`

| Good | Bad |
|------|-----|
| "Lyka is looking to identify and leverage opportunities where AI and automation can improve operational efficiency across its core business operations teams" | "Situation overview" |
| "TCP has identified opportunities where AI and automation can improve operational efficiency" | "Current state of operations" |
| "Manual AP processing is consuming 26 hours per week across three teams" | "AP processing challenges" |

**Rules:**
- Must be a complete statement with a subject, verb, and object
- Name the client
- Name the specific scope (which teams, which function, which process)
- State the direction or aspiration, not just the problem
- No fluff: avoid "looking to explore", "keen to understand", "various opportunities"
- One sentence. If it needs two, the second belongs in the body.

---

## Body content

The body fills in the evidence behind the action title. Two jobs:

**Paragraph 1 — Who and what:** Establish the client's identity, scale, and relevant context. Name the teams, tools, or functions in scope.

**Paragraph 2+ — Why it matters:** Describe the specific pain points, inefficiencies, or opportunities. Be concrete — name tools, quantify where possible, describe the friction.

**Selective bold:** Bold the client name, team names, tool names, and the most critical problem phrases. Bold draws the reader's eye to the facts that matter. Do not bold entire sentences.

| Bold this | Don't bold this |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Lyka** | "Lyka is a leading..." (whole sentence) |
| **People Operations, Business Operations, and Customer Care** | "three teams" (vague) |
| **EstimateOne**, **HubSpot**, **Excel**, **Tradify** | "multiple tools" (unnamed) |
| **disconnected systems and manual data entry** | "some challenges" (vague) |

**Avoid:**
- Vague claims without evidence ("significant inefficiencies", "various challenges")
- Naming the client as "the company" or "the organisation" — use their name
- Describing Turing Works' capabilities — that belongs on objectives, not here
- Listing more than 3–4 pain points — group them into themes if there are many

---

## Source citation

Always present. Names the person and context where the information came from.

Format: `"Source: [Type of interaction] with [Client] [Role/Name] ([Person Name])"`

Examples:
- "Source: Initial discussions with Lyka ELT Member (Mary Dong)"
- "Source: Discovery workshop with TCP leadership, January 2026"

---

## Expanded variant (with callout boxes)

When the situation slide uses callout boxes (e.g. one per team/function in scope):

- Each box gets a **bold heading** (team or function name, 10pt navy)
- Each box gets a **one-sentence description** of that area's pain point (8.5pt grey)
- Boxes should be parallel in structure — same grammatical pattern across all three
- A "Focus" label can mark which area is the primary engagement scope
