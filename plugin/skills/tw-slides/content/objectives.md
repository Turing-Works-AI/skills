# Content Guide: Objectives Slide

These guidelines tier down from the slide craft rules in SKILL.md. They govern what goes on this slide, not how it looks. For visual spec, see `components/objectives/spec.md`.

---

## Purpose

The objectives slide answers: "What will we do about it, and why now?" It bridges the situation (where the client is) to the workplan (how we'll get there). The reader should finish this slide understanding the 2–3 focus areas and why each one matters.

---

## Section subtitle (left panel)

Format: `"How Turing Works will help [Client] [verb] [outcome]"`

Examples from actual decks:
- "How Turing Works will help Lyka unlock AI capability"
- "How Turing Works will help Lyka implement new AP processes"

**Rules:**
- Always starts with "How Turing Works will help"
- Names the client
- Uses a concrete verb (unlock, implement, streamline, reduce, automate) — not "explore" or "investigate"
- States the outcome, not the activity

---

## Action title (bold teal headline)

The action title frames why the engagement is timely and what makes it a strong opportunity. It connects the situation to the work — not by restating the problem, but by identifying the opening.

**Pattern:** `"[Client context or recent change] presents [opportunity/need] to [outcome]"`

| Good | Bad |
|------|-----|
| "Lyka's recent consolidation of HR tooling into Rippling and planned investment in a dedicated People Ops Manager present a strong opportunity to build AI capability and generate operational leverage" | "Objectives for the engagement" |
| "There are a number of operational pain points and opportunities for automation" | "What we will do" |

**Rules:**
- Must reference something specific about the client's context — a recent change, a planned investment, a known pain point
- Frames the work as an opportunity, not just a reaction to problems
- One to two sentences maximum
- No fluff: avoid "excited to partner", "uniquely positioned", "comprehensive approach"

---

## Body content

The body describes 2–3 named focus areas. Each focus area is a distinct workstream or capability that Turing Works will deliver.

**Structure:**

```
[Contextual setup sentence — optional, links action title to focus areas]

[Focus area 1 bold heading:] Description of what this workstream covers,
what it will produce, and why it matters to the client.

[Focus area 2 bold heading:] Description of what this workstream covers,
what it will produce, and why it matters to the client.
```

**Focus area headings** — bold, embedded in the paragraph text (not separate shapes). These become the workstream names that carry through to the workplan.

Examples from actual decks:
- **AI Workshops and Capability Building:** "We will equip the People Team to become AI natives..."
- **AI Automations Assessment:** "We will map the current People Ops tech stack and workflows..."
- **Quoting & Proposal Generation:** "The process for creating quotes from tenders is time-consuming, requiring 10+ hours per quote..."
- **Payment Orders and Invoicing in Tradify:** "The recreation of Excel quotes (100+ items) into Tradify is manual..."

**Rules for focus areas:**
- 2–3 focus areas per slide. More than 3 means the scope isn't focused enough.
- Each heading should be a noun phrase naming the workstream (not a sentence, not a verb phrase)
- Each description should name specific tools, processes, or quantities where known
- Focus areas should be MECE — no overlap between them, collectively covering the engagement scope
- The focus area names used here must match the workstream row labels on the workplan slide

**Selective bold:** Bold the focus area heading names and key concrete terms (tool names, quantities, specific deliverables). Do not bold entire paragraphs.

**Avoid:**
- Generic descriptions ("we will assess your operations") — name the specific function or tool
- Promising outcomes without naming the method ("improve efficiency by 40%") — state what you'll do
- Using "leverage", "unlock", "transform" in body text — these are acceptable in the action title but become fluff when repeated
- Describing the client's problems again — that was the situation slide. Here, describe what you'll do about them.

## Source citation

Format: `"Source: Turing Works statement of work hypothesis"` or `"Source: Turing Works engagement scope, [Month Year]"`

This is always a Turing Works source (not a client source) because the objectives represent TW's proposed approach, not the client's stated needs.
