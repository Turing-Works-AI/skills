# Writing Polish Skill

## Overview

**Purpose:** Refine LinkedIn posts, articles, and professional content while preserving your voice. Focus on hooks, flow, and credibility without rewriting from scratch.

**Core Principles:**
- Show, don't rewrite: Display original → suggested improvement → rationale
- Preserve authenticity: Flag LLM-isms, maintain your analytical tone
- Strengthen hooks: Apply proven formats, add specificity (people, pain, payoff, proof)
- Validate credibility: Check for trust signals and measurable results

---

## Process

### 1. Initial Analysis
When you provide content, I will:
- Identify the **hook** (first 1-2 sentences)
- Check for **credibility markers** (timestamps, metrics, specific examples)
- Flag any **LLM-isms** or weak phrasing
- Note the **sign-off** strategy (or lack thereof)

### 2. Hook Strengthening

I'll test your hook against these criteria:
- **Immediate value:** Does it state the benefit upfront?
- **Proof/specificity:** Numbers, timeframes, or concrete results?
- **PPP formula:** People + Pain + Payoff present?
- **Format match:** Does it use a proven structure?

**Suggested formats to try:**
```
✓ Timeline opener: "Last week, I..."
✓ Unexpected turn: Purpose sentence → surprise sentence
✓ Dream outcome: "How to X without Y"
✓ Best way: "The best way to {dream outcome}"
✓ Timeboxed achievement: "How I X in Y timeframe"
✓ Struggle list: "My biggest struggles as a {type of person} were:"
✓ Weird contrast: "It's weird {people} still use {tool} to {outcome}"
✓ Experience anchor: "I've been {action} for {timeframe}"
```

**Hook revision example:**
```
Original: "I built an automation that saves time."
Suggested: "Last week I delivered a 100% accurate invoice extraction automation."
Why: Adds timeline, specificity (100%), and proof of completion. Teases "how" to drive read-through.
```

### 3. Flow & Clarity Review

For each paragraph or section needing work, I'll show:

```
Original:
[Your sentence/paragraph]

Suggested:
[Refined version]

Why:
[Concise explanation of change]
```

**What I'll fix:**
- Passive → active voice
- Vague → specific claims
- Long → punchy sentences (where appropriate)
- Redundant phrasing
- Weak transitions

### 4. Credibility Check

I'll flag missing or weak credibility signals:
- ❌ "Many companies struggle with this" → ⚠️ Generic claim
- ✓ "After 37 client calls, I found 3 patterns" → ✓ Specific proof

**Credibility booster checklist:**
- [ ] Specific timeframe mentioned?
- [ ] Measurable result included?
- [ ] Personal experience/proof shown?
- [ ] Names/tools/methods cited?

**Credibility enhancers:**
- Tiny time-stamps: "Built in 48 hours." "After 37 calls." "Q3, €12.4k saved."
- Specific time windows feel real
- Measurable results: "25x mentions" "100% accuracy" "70% of time spent"
- Money saved/earned with specificity

### 5. Sign-Off Strengthening

Your examples show strong callbacks and question-based endings. I'll ensure your sign-off:
- Reinforces the main point
- Creates engagement (question/challenge/emotion)
- Feels natural, not formulaic

**Sign-off options:**
- **Callback to hook** - Re-iterate the opening
- **Yes/no question** - Simple, engagement-driving
- **Motivational line** - Inspirational close
- **Abrupt ending** - Catch reader off guard
- **Funny comment** - Light, memorable
- **Single emoji** - (used sparingly)
- **Challenge/invitation** - Ask for their experience

**Key principle:** Last line read = last thing they remember. People forget the point by the end, so remind them or the message gets lost. Emotional endings create action.

## Anti-Patterns (LLM-isms to Avoid)

I'll flag and suggest alternatives for:
- Em dashes (—) → Use colons, semicolons, or periods
- "Let's delve into..." → "Here's what I found..."
- "In the world of..." → Cut it entirely
- "It's not just about X, it's about Y..." → "X matters less than Y"
- "Unlocking the potential..." → "Making X work for Y"
- "At the end of the day..." → "Ultimately" or cut
- Overuse of "quietly" → Remove or use "without fanfare"

---

## Style Guidelines

**Tone:** Expert, analytical, direct. Think *Economist* meets LinkedIn thought leader.

**Structure:**
- Use numbered lists (1️⃣ 2️⃣ 3️⃣) for steps/techniques
- Bold key phrases sparingly
- Keep paragraphs short (1-3 sentences)
- Use line breaks for readability

**Voice markers from your examples:**
- Short, punchy sentences: "How? I spent 70% of my time testing."
- Direct questions to reader: "What else have you found works?"
- Personal timeline anchors: "Last week..." "In 20 minutes..."
- Metrics as proof: "100% accurate" "25 slides" "37 calls"
- Conversational authority: "Paradigm. I hate the word paradigm."

## Reference Examples

These examples represent your style and should guide all suggestions. You'll add to these over time.

### Example 1: Invoice Extraction Post

```
Last week I delivered a 100% accurate invoice extraction automation. How?

I spent 70% of my time testing, using three techniques:

1️⃣ 𝐄𝐯𝐚𝐥𝐮𝐚𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧𝐬 𝐮𝐬𝐢𝐧𝐠 𝐥𝐚𝐛𝐞𝐥𝐥𝐞𝐝 𝐝𝐚𝐭𝐚
I created a test batch of invoices with known correct outputs. I then iterated the agent until it hit 100% accuracy. Bonus: the same tests let me re-validate after I change the model or add fields.

2️⃣ 𝐑𝐞𝐚𝐝 𝐢𝐭𝐬 𝐦𝐢𝐧𝐝
Reasoning models give summaries of their thought process. Checking often finds reasonable reasons the AI got it wrong (e.g. duplication, formatting, derivation of numbers). You then tell it what to do in those situations.

3️⃣ 𝐄𝐝𝐠𝐞 𝐜𝐚𝐬𝐞𝐬
Like humans, AI can struggle when weird stuff happens. I gave it the unusual cases in testing (complex PDFs, multiple attachments, long email threads).

What else have you found works? Or have you missed in testing?…
```

**What works:**
- Strong hook: Timeline + specific result (100% accurate)
- Credibility: 70% time spent = proof of thoroughness
- Structure: Three clear techniques with emoji numbering
- Sign-off: Question inviting engagement and shared learning

### Example 2: OpenAI Atlas Post

```
Paradigm. I hate the word paradigm.

And yet OpenAI's Atlas feels like a new paradigm in browsing the internet.

Paradigm is an abused word. When something changes a little, it gets used a lot.

But Atlas is the biggest change in how I browse the internet since I escaped AOL's walled garden onto the World Wide Web.

The old way we browse is search first. Atlas is AI first. In simple terms, the search bar becomes the AI chat bar.

I'm finding information faster. Instead of the first page of Google, an enshittified mess of ads and SEO, you get a summary of what you need to know.

When you go deeper? Search used to end once you clicked into a site. With Atlas, you keep chatting. ChatGPT sits in the sidebar, ready for follow-up questions.

There are even glimmers of autonomous browsing through Agent Mode. Though for now it feels like watching your parents use the internet complete with ponderous thought process, slow cursor movements, and occasionally giving up.

My only criticism is the app icon... It's dark and loses the "explore the web" design of other browsers. So when Chrome was still open I found myself teleported back by keyboard muscle memory.

Curious if others feel the same shift?
```

**What works:**
- Hook: Contrarian opener (hate paradigm → but this is one)
- Conversational authority: Short sentences, personal observations
- Analogies: "watching your parents use the internet"
- Balanced: Includes criticism (app icon) not just praise
- Sign-off: Simple question inviting shared experience
